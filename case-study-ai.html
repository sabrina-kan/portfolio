Teaching a machine to govern content

How I designed an AI-powered review system to scale content governance across a growing team — all without adding process overhead.

AI strategy 2026 – ongoing Content design lead
My role Content design lead
Timeline 2026 – ongoing
Deliverables AI agent, evaluation framework, governance model

Project overview

As the content design team scaled from one to six people, informal content quality processes stopped being viable. Reviews were inconsistent. Style guide adherence was dependent on whoever happened to review a given string. And with a 20-product platform and ~50 contributors outside the team, we had no way to catch issues upstream.

The solution: build an AI-powered content review agent that could encode the team's standards, flag issues at scale, and free up human review cycles for the judgment calls that actually require a person.

The problem

Manual content reviews don't scale. As the team grew, I noticed three patterns:

  • Inconsistency at the edges. Contributors outside the content team weren't catching style issues because they didn't know the rules. By the time strings reached review, dozens of decisions had already been made.
  • Review bottlenecks. Every piece of UI copy required a content designer sign-off. That worked when there was one product. It broke down at ten.
  • Style guide drift. The style guide existed, but it wasn't operationalized. Writers referenced it inconsistently, and no one had time to audit every release.

The constraint

We couldn't add headcount. The ask was to scale governance without adding process overhead — meaning whatever we built had to be low-friction to use and easy to maintain.

That ruled out more checklists, more review stages, or any solution that required a human to do more work. The system needed to do the work.

The insight

Most content review tasks aren't judgment calls — they're pattern matching. Capitalization, punctuation, tone markers, prohibited terms, button label conventions: these are rules, not taste. And rules can be encoded.

If I could separate the rule-based work from the judgment-based work, I could automate the former and focus the team on the latter.

The question wasn't whether to use AI — it was what to teach it, and how to know if it was learning the right things.

The approach

1

Structuring the knowledge base

Before any model could be built, I had to make our standards machine-readable. I audited the existing style guide and categorized every rule by type: prescriptive (always do X), prohibitive (never do Y), and contextual (do X when Z).

Contextual rules were the hardest to encode — they required me to make implicit knowledge explicit. This surfaced gaps in the style guide itself, which I documented and resolved as I went.

2

Sequencing the content architecture

I designed the agent's review logic in layers: surface-level checks first (spelling, casing, punctuation), then structural checks (label length, parallel construction, empty state completeness), then tone and clarity checks last.

Sequencing mattered because later checks are only meaningful if earlier ones pass. A tone flag on a string with a spelling error is noise — it dilutes signal and erodes trust in the system.

3

Building the evaluation framework

With any AI system, the real design challenge is evaluation: how do you know it's working? I built a test set of ~200 strings — a mix of real production copy and intentionally flawed examples — and defined pass/fail criteria for each check.

We run iterative quality benchmarking cycles, tracking precision and recall by check type. When the agent flags something a human wouldn't, we investigate whether the rule needs refinement or the agent needs retraining. The evaluation framework keeps the human-in-the-loop without making every decision human-dependent.

The outcomes

1

Content review coverage at scale

The agent now runs on every content submission across the platform, catching rule-based issues before they reach a human reviewer. The team's review time shifted from catching mechanical errors to making strategic decisions.

2

A living style guide

The process of making our standards machine-readable also made them clearer to humans. The structured rule taxonomy is now used as the canonical reference for onboarding new writers and resolving style disagreements.

3

A replicable governance model

The framework — structured knowledge base, layered review logic, test-set evaluation — is designed to be replicable. Other teams at the company have expressed interest in adapting it for localization QA and accessibility checks.
