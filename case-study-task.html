Zero-to-One: A Collaborative Task App

Defining the language of an entirely new product — from IA and naming conventions to empty states and launch-day copy.

End-to-End SaaS 2023 Content Designer
My Role Content Designer (sole)
Timeline 20 weeks (Q2–Q4 2023)
Team 2 PMs, 4 Designers, 6 Engineers

The Challenge

I joined Patchwork — a startup building a collaborative task management tool for remote teams — at the very beginning of product development. There was no existing product, no copy to inherit, and no content precedent to follow.

That's both the best and most exposed position for a content designer to be in. Every decision matters, nothing is set in stone, and the product team is genuinely uncertain about what to build. My job was to be the voice of language in every room I walked into.

What I Did

1

Discovery & concept definition

Participated in product discovery workshops, helping the team define what "collaborative task management" actually meant for their target users. Surfaced language risks early: the word "task" meant different things to different user segments.

2

Information architecture

Led the IA work: card sorting with 20 target users, tree testing with 50 participants, and multiple rounds of navigation restructuring before the first wireframe was produced.

3

Naming & taxonomy

Developed the product's complete object model — what things are called, how they relate to each other, and what verbs apply to each. Published as a content spec that design and engineering referenced throughout development.

4

Copy for the full product

Wrote all UI copy across onboarding, core task flows, settings, notifications, empty states, and error messages — working directly in Figma with design tokens for copy.

5

Help content foundation

Authored 18 help articles covering the key use cases, formatted for both the in-app help widget and the external knowledge base.

The IA Work in Detail

The navigation was the hardest problem. The product had three distinct user roles — individual contributors, team leads, and admins — each with a different mental model of what the product was for. A single navigation structure had to serve all three.

We ran two rounds of tree testing before we found a structure that worked. The key insight: admins think in terms of "settings and permissions" while contributors think in terms of "my work." We separated these into two distinct navigation areas rather than trying to merge them into a single hierarchy.

Results

4.2/5 Average usability score across 30 sessions of launch-day testing — the highest the research team had seen on a v1 product.
0 Critical content bugs filed in the first 30 days post-launch (tracking terminology inconsistencies, confusing labels, broken flows).
82% Onboarding completion rate in the first cohort of 400 users — well above the 60% benchmark the PM had set as a success target.

What I Learned

Zero-to-one work is not glamorous. A lot of it is attending meetings where decisions get made and unmade, and quietly advocating for language to be treated as a design material rather than an afterthought.

The most valuable thing I did wasn't writing the copy — it was building a shared vocabulary with the team. When everyone uses the same words to talk about the product internally, the copy that ends up in the product is more consistent. The object model document became one of the most-referenced artefacts in the repo.
